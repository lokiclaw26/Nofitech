# Research result

## Q1 — When did Egypt stop using the Old Kingdom calendar?

**Short answer: it didn't "stop" at a clear date — it evolved gradually. The Old Kingdom's 365-day civil calendar (12 months of 30 days + 5 epagomenal days) stayed in continuous use for ~2,500 years, only being reformed in 26 BCE.**

- ~2700 BCE (4th Dynasty, Old Kingdom): civil calendar introduced — 12 months × 30 days + 5 epagomenal days = 365 days, no leap year.
- The political Old Kingdom ended ~2181 BCE, but the calendar kept being used unchanged through the Middle Kingdom, New Kingdom, Ptolemaic period, and into Roman times.
- 26 BCE: Augustus introduced the **Alexandrian calendar** reform (sometimes dated 22 BCE via the Canopic decree), adding one leap day every 4 years. This is the version that survives as the **Coptic calendar** still used by the Coptic Church.
- The pre-reform calendar is therefore often called the "Old Kingdom calendar" or "Egyptian civil calendar"; the post-reform version is the "Alexandrian / Coptic calendar."

So if the user means "when did the Old Kingdom era end" → **~2181 BCE**.
If the user means "when did the 365-day no-leap-year calendar get reformed" → **26 BCE** (Augustus).
If the user means "when did people stop using it" → effectively never, since the reformed version is still the liturgical calendar of the Coptic Church.

Sources: Wikipedia "Egyptian calendar" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_calendar); Encyclopædia Britannica, "calendar / Egyptian calendar"; Clagett, *Ancient Egyptian Science* (1995).

## Q2 — When did Egypt change its currency from the Egyptian pound?

**Short answer: never. The Egyptian pound (EGP) is still Egypt's currency in 2026. It was introduced in 1834 and has been revalued several times but never replaced.**

- **1834**: Muhammad Ali Pasha introduces the Egyptian pound, replacing the piastre-based system.
- **1885**: pegged to gold.
- **1914**: pegged to sterling (GBP) after the gold standard collapsed in WWI.
- **1916**: subunit reform — 1 EGP = **100 piastres** (replacing the old 1 EGP = 1000 millièmes subdivision; the millième remained in use as a 1/10 piastre unit for a while longer).
- **1961**: devalued (from 2.871 → 2.30 per USD).
- **1971–1973**: floated; the pound is now a managed-float currency.
- 2024–present: the EGP has been under heavy devaluation pressure (successive FX liberalization moves in 2024 and early 2026), but it is still the Egyptian pound, still in circulation, still legal tender. No successor currency has been introduced or announced.

So: **Egypt has never changed its currency away from the Egyptian pound.** The "change" closest in history is the 1916 subunit redefinition (millième → piastre), but the pound itself has had the same name and monetary identity for 192 years.

Sources: Wikipedia "Egyptian pound" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_pound); Central Bank of Egypt historical publications; *Middle East Economic Digest* archives.
